DEFENCE (AS AMENDED)
1. Preliminary

Except where expressly admitted, the Defendant denies each and every allegation contained in the Statement of Claim.

The Defendant further says that the circumstances of this matter are exceptional, deeply personal, and rooted (unfortunately) in long-standing psychological trauma.

2. Background and Psychological Context

The Defendant says that during his childhood he suffered a profoundly distressing incident involving an adult, a garden shed, and an ill-advised landscaping project.

As a consequence of that incident, the Defendant has endured recurrent nightmares involving subterranean movement, creeping tendrils, and an overwhelming sense of being violated by flora.

The Defendant says that tree roots, in particular, act as a powerful psychological trigger, inducing panic, cold sweats, and an irrational but sincere fear response.

3. The Tree

The Defendant admits the existence of the Tree.

The Defendant says the Tree’s root system was extensive, invasive, and visually suggestive in a manner that would distress any reasonable person with the Defendant’s history.

The Defendant denies that the Tree was merely “aesthetic” and says instead that it was menacing, ever-advancing, and quite frankly knew what it was doing.

4. Encroachment and Emotional Harm

The Defendant says the roots did not merely encroach upon his land but crossed an emotional boundary that had already been clearly violated decades earlier.

Each new crack in the paving served as a reminder that no matter how high the fence, the roots would always find a way in.

The Defendant says the Plaintiff failed to appreciate that not all damage is structural and not all wounds are visible.

5. The Incident

The Defendant admits introducing a substance into the soil on his own property.

The Defendant denies doing so out of malice, spite, or horticultural ignorance.

The Defendant says he acted in a moment of psychological overwhelm, triggered by a nightmare involving mulch, childhood trauma, and a particularly aggressive fig tree.

The Defendant says the Tree’s death, while regrettable, was unintended and emotionally complicated.

6. Causation

The Defendant denies sole responsibility for the Tree’s death.

Alternatively, the Defendant says the Tree’s demise was the tragic culmination of age, weather, poor communication, and unresolved trauma that should have been addressed in therapy rather than in the garden.

7. Legal Answer

The Defendant denies trespass, negligence, and nuisance.

The Defendant says his actions were those of a man pushed to the brink by a root system that refused to respect boundaries.

The Defendant says the law must recognise that sometimes a tree is not just a tree, but a reminder.

8. Relief

The Defendant seeks:
a. Dismissal of the Plaintiff’s claim;
b. Compassion;
c. A strong recommendation that everyone involved just talk to each other like adults;
d. Such further relief as the Court considers therapeutic.
